The first step we took towards systematically solving the domain curation challenge was to establish the various actors involved in and around domain curation, then focus on people who are users of current systems which try to enable domain curation. Through a structured user goal identification activity we modeled system users (roles not personas),  their key goals and tasks we believe they should undertake in an ideal system.


Why is domain curation a challenge?


Domains are a data classification mechanism. They represent discrete business concepts — and mappings to physical implementations of those concepts. Physical database columns representing concepts such as dates-of-birth, social-security-numbers, product-codes, etc. are represented differently in different databases. As such, the larger the data landscape, the more challenging it becomes to understand the purpose and value of the data it contains.

User Goals

Based on the user goals identified in the previous step, we created a simple architecture to map the goals and tasks into screens and broad functionality.


The idea was to use this understanding as a bridge between the usage model (users, goals, tasks) and the designs that would subsequently shape the software systems.


Laying out our understanding in this fashion also enabled us to ensure that the functionality wasn't running too broad or deep and was available in coherent bite-sized chunks for each user role.





Information Architecture

Another vantage point we looked at from was that of the workflow between various system users. This perspective facilitates a better understanding of how the different pieces of the system would come together. It also helps to ensure that activity loops are getting closed.


Key artefacts are identified and so are any breakages in the flow of work between different users.


Such a representation also helps clarify nomenclature. The same set of tasks might be done by people with different job titles across organisations.

The domain curation process seeks to train an automated data discovery and classification system to accurately classify physical data elements according to the business concept they represent. This is envisioned as a three part process, one part discovery, one part specification and one part validation. Domains are defined in terms of representative examples, common value patterns, and possible synonyms or terms used to name a particular type of data. The more complete the definition, the more accurate the automated classification system becomes. The key challenge is to optimize this discovery, specification and validation process.


What do we stand to gain by improving the domain curation process?


The poster above seeks to answer this question. It identifies the people (roles) who are involved in the domain curation process today, what we understand their key goals to be, and what tasks they perform today to reach those goals. Our design objective is to build tools that enable each stakeholder to accomplish their respective goals in an optimum fashion. Understanding the necessary tasks actors in an ideal system should undertake is the first step towards systematically solving the domain curation challenge.
